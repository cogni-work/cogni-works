## S — Solutions: Digital Foundation

### ACT Horizon (0-2 Years)

#### 1. Legacy Barriers & Migration

**Trend Overview** — Legacy systems remain the critical barrier to digital transformation, with more than 75% of enterprises now using AI as part of their modernization strategy [Riseup Labs](https://riseuplabs.com/legacy-system-modernization-trends/). Nearly 60% of AI leaders view legacy-system integration as the primary barrier to agentic AI adoption. GenAI is cutting modernization project timelines by up to 50%, handling 69–75% of code edits during large-scale migrations [Devox Software](https://devoxsoftware.com/blog/the-2026-legacy-modernization-report-research-insights-and-strategic-roadmap/).

**Implications** — Utilities with 20+ year IT landscapes face particularly acute migration challenges, as operational technology systems often lack API compatibility and modern security architectures.

**Opportunities** — AI-assisted migration reduces risk and timeline for legacy modernization programs.

**Recommended Actions** — 1. Develop legacy modernization strategy with API-first architecture. 2. Leverage GenAI tools for accelerated code migration. 3. Implement phased migration with parallel operations.

#### 2. AI/ML Engineer Hiring (88% Growth)

**Trend Overview** — AI/ML hiring grew 88% year-on-year, with AI/ML Engineer representing 45% of all AI/ML job titles [Second Talent](https://www.secondtalent.com/resources/most-in-demand-ai-engineering-skills-and-salary-ranges/). **Implications** — Utilities compete with tech companies for scarce AI talent, requiring competitive compensation packages and compelling mission narratives.

**Opportunities** — Utilities can differentiate through purpose-driven career narratives around climate and infrastructure impact.

**Recommended Actions** — 1. Develop competitive AI talent acquisition strategies. 2. Build internal AI upskilling programs. 3. Establish partnerships with universities for talent pipelines.

#### 4. Talent Gap: 310,000 by 2030

**Trend Overview** — The power industry may need more than 750,000 new workers by 2030 [Goldman Sachs Research]. More than 30% of utility employees are within five years of retirement, with nuclear- and grid-related professions facing the steepest challenges — retirements outnumbering new entrants by ratios of 1.7:1 [Power Magazine](https://www.powermag.com/bridging-the-gap-how-the-power-industry-is-tackling-its-workforce-crisis/). **Implications** — The converging talent shortage and retirement wave force utilities to simultaneously automate operations and develop new workforce capabilities.

**Opportunities** — Automation and AI can compensate for workforce shortages while upskilling programs prepare remaining staff for digital roles.

**Recommended Actions** — 1. Develop automation strategies for critical workforce shortage areas. 2. Build remote work and flexible deployment models. 3. Launch apprenticeship and upskilling programs.
