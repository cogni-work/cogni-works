## T — Trends: External Effects

### ACT Horizon (0-2 Years)

#### 1. Data Center Power Demand Surge

**Trend Overview** — Data center expansion and AI workloads are driving electricity demand growth at unprecedented rates. U.S. data center demand is projected to reach 75.8 GW in 2026, with data centers expected to consume 6.7–12% of total U.S. electricity by 2028 [S&P Global](https://www.spglobal.com/energy/en/news-research/latest-news/electric-power/101425-data-center-grid-power-demand-to-rise-22-in-2025-nearly-triple-by-2030). In Europe, similar dynamics are at play as hyperscaler expansion accelerates. Grid capacity planning by utilities has surged, with five-year peak demand forecasts jumping from 38 GW to 128 GW between 2023 and 2024 [WRI](https://www.wri.org/insights/us-data-centers-electricity-demand).

**Implications** — German utilities face a critical maturity gap between the rapid innovation cycles of data center technologies and the slow, linear deployment pace of the electrical grid infrastructure. Interconnection queues for new generation and storage projects continue to lengthen.

**Opportunities** — Utilities can position as preferred power partners for hyperscalers by offering flexible load contracts and demand-response programs. Early movers will capture long-term revenue streams from co-located generation assets.

**Recommended Actions** — 1. Accelerate grid capacity planning with 10-year demand scenarios incorporating AI workload growth. 2. Develop dedicated data center power supply programs with flexible pricing. 3. Invest in grid-enhancing technologies to unlock existing capacity faster.

#### 2. EU AI Act Compliance

**Trend Overview** — The EU AI Act takes effect on 2 August 2026, requiring energy utilities to establish comprehensive AI governance frameworks. All operators of high-risk AI systems must implement documented risk management, robust data governance, technical documentation, automatic logging, human oversight, and cybersecurity safeguards [Orrick](https://www.orrick.com/en/Insights/2025/11/The-EU-AI-Act-6-Steps-to-Take-Before-2-August-2026). AI embedded in power grids is classified as particularly sensitive, with disruptions risking catastrophic failures in electricity, water, and gas systems [Futurium](https://futurium.ec.europa.eu/en/apply-ai-alliance/community-content/algorithms-energy-compliance-novel-governance-framework-eu-ai-act-2026-enforcement-high-risk-energy).

**Implications** — Utilities deploying AI for grid optimization, predictive maintenance, or customer service must complete conformity assessments, finalize technical documentation, and register high-risk systems before the August 2026 deadline.

**Opportunities** — Early compliance creates competitive advantage and enables faster AI deployment for operational efficiency gains estimated at 30–50%.

**Recommended Actions** — 1. Classify all AI systems and assess high-risk categories immediately. 2. Establish an AI governance board with cross-functional oversight. 3. Build compliance documentation templates for rapid rollout across business units.

#### 3. Smart Grid Cybersecurity

**Trend Overview** — The global grid cybersecurity market is projected to grow from $9.11 billion in 2025 to $10.43 billion in 2026, reflecting a 14.4% CAGR, and is predicted to reach $29.70 billion by 2034 [Precedence Research](https://www.precedenceresearch.com/grid-cybersecurity-market). Zero Trust Architecture (ZTA) is gaining traction as the predominant security paradigm for critical energy infrastructure, requiring continuous authentication and micro-segmentation across grid environments [GM Insights](https://www.gminsights.com/industry-analysis/smart-grid-cybersecurity-market).

**Implications** — National cybersecurity standards mandate real-time threat detection, endpoint security, and incident response systems for KRITIS infrastructure. Non-compliance risks regulatory penalties and operational disruptions.

**Opportunities** — Utilities that invest early in zero-trust architectures can leverage security maturity for performance-based regulation advantages and reduced insurance premiums.

**Recommended Actions** — 1. Implement network segmentation between IT and OT environments. 2. Deploy AI-powered threat detection with continuous monitoring. 3. Establish a dedicated Security Operations Center for grid infrastructure.

#### 4. Energy Sharing Regulation

**Trend Overview** — By 17 July 2026, new EU rules on energy sharing take effect, enabling multiple suppliers for individual premises and facilitating decentralized electricity trading [Exnaton](https://www.exnaton.ai/en/blog/energy-market-europe-2026). Countries including Portugal, France, Spain, Italy, and Austria have advanced regulatory environments, while Germany is moving toward dynamic energy sharing coefficients.

**Implications** — Utilities must develop platform capabilities for peer-to-peer trading, real-time billing, and decentralized settlement. Customer relationships will fundamentally shift from supplier-consumer to platform-participant.

**Opportunities** — Energy sharing platforms create new revenue streams through transaction fees, value-added services, and customer data monetization.

**Recommended Actions** — 1. Develop P2P trading platform capabilities with blockchain settlement. 2. Build real-time billing infrastructure for multi-supplier premises. 3. Launch pilot programs in energy communities before July 2026 deadline.

#### 5. Renewable Curtailment Management

**Trend Overview** — Solar curtailment in Germany surged 97% year-on-year in 2024, reaching 1,389 GWh, while approximately 8% of solar energy was deliberately cut in H1 2025 — more than double the prior year [Strategic Energy Europe](https://strategicenergy.eu/solar-curtailment-germany/). Wind and solar power led German public electricity generation for the first time in 2025, with renewables reaching 55.9% of net public generation [Fraunhofer ISE](https://www.ise.fraunhofer.de/en/press-media/press-releases/2026/german-public-electricity-generation-in-2025-wind-and-solar-power-take-the-lead.html).

**Implications** — Distribution networks now account for 26% of congestion events (up from 20% in 2023), indicating that curtailment challenges are spreading beyond the transmission grid.

**Opportunities** — Automated demand-response systems and battery storage coordination can monetize surplus renewable generation instead of curtailing it.

**Recommended Actions** — 1. Deploy automated load management systems at congestion-prone nodes. 2. Invest in behind-the-meter storage at distribution substations. 3. Develop dynamic curtailment algorithms that optimize across wind and solar assets.

### PLAN Horizon (2-5 Years)

#### 1. CCfD Expansion 2026-2027

**Trend Overview** — Germany has launched a €6 billion Carbon Contracts for Difference auction for 2026, now expanded to include CCS, CCU, hydrogen, and industrial heat pumps [Fuel Cells Works](https://fuelcellsworks.com/2025/10/06/green-investment/germany-opens-pre-bid-phase-for-6bn-2026-climate-contracts-tender-adds-ccs-and-hydrogen-scope). CCfDs lock in price guarantees for up to 15 years, shielding investors from volatile CO2 and energy markets. Germany may set aside roughly €50 billion for CCfD subsidies overall [Hydrogen Insight](https://www.hydrogeninsight.com/policy/hydrogen-in-industry-germany-to-set-aside-roughly-50bn-for-carbon-contracts-for-difference-subsidy-scheme/2-1-1462051).

**Implications** — The expanded scope creates investment certainty for hard-to-abate sectors including chemicals, steel, cement, and ceramics — all major utility customers.

**Opportunities** — Utilities can position as green hydrogen suppliers and CCfD project developers, capturing long-term revenue streams backed by government guarantees.

**Recommended Actions** — 1. Evaluate H2 production economics and integration scenarios. 2. Develop financing structures aligned with CCfD auction requirements. 3. Partner with industrial customers for joint CCfD bids.

#### 2. Electricity Market Reform 2026

**Trend Overview** — Germany is replacing its current market premium model with bilateral contracts for difference (CfDs) while abolishing the fixed feed-in tariff [Clean Energy Wire](https://www.cleanenergywire.org/news/preview-2026-germany-should-use-electricity-market-reform-efficient-renewables-additions-researcher). The Bundesnetzagentur's AgNeS initiative proposes time- and location-varying grid fees, with preliminary 2026 tariffs showing an average drop from 6.65 to 2.86 ct/kWh (-57%) [Trio Advisory](https://www.trioadvisory.com/resources/grid-fee-subsidy-and-power-tax-cut-germany-2026-what-energy-intensive-sites-will-really-save).

**Implications** — The shift to time-variable pricing and direct marketing obligations fundamentally changes utility revenue models and requires sophisticated pricing engines.

**Opportunities** — Dynamic tariffs create opportunities for demand flexibility platforms and energy-as-a-service offerings.

**Recommended Actions** — 1. Develop PPA and CfD contracting capabilities. 2. Invest in dynamic pricing and billing infrastructure. 3. Build customer communication strategies for new tariff structures.

#### 4. Energy Efficiency Directive ISO 50001

**Trend Overview** — The EU Energy Efficiency Directive mandates that large utilities achieve ISO 50001 certification and conduct regular energy audits by October 2027 [Wirtek](https://www.wirtek.com/blog/energy-efficiency-directive-eu-company-obligations-from-2026-to-2030). This directive establishes binding energy efficiency targets and requires energy management systems across all major European utilities.

**Implications** — Utilities must invest in energy management systems, internal audit capabilities, and continuous improvement processes to meet the October 2027 deadline.

**Opportunities** — ISO 50001 certification drives operational efficiency gains and positions utilities favorably for regulatory incentives.

**Recommended Actions** — 1. Launch ISO 50001 certification projects immediately. 2. Establish internal energy audit routines and improvement cycles. 3. Integrate energy management into existing operational excellence programs.

### OBSERVE Horizon (5+ Years)

#### 1. €500 Billion Infrastructure Fund

**Trend Overview** — Germany's debt-financed special fund of €500 billion covers infrastructure and climate neutrality projects over the next decade, with approximately €100 billion allocated to the Climate and Transformation Fund and €58 billion planned for deployment in 2026 alone [Clean Energy Wire](https://www.cleanenergywire.org/factsheets/qa-germanys-eu500-bln-infrastructure-fund-whats-it-climate-and-energy). The fund provides grid fee relief and supports geothermal energy, offshore wind, and energy infrastructure modernization [Federal Ministry of Finance](https://www.bundesfinanzministerium.de/Web/EN/Issues/Public-Finances/SVIK/special-fund-infrastructure-and-climate-neutrality.html).

**Implications** — This represents the largest single infrastructure investment in German history, creating unprecedented opportunities for utility modernization and grid expansion.

**Opportunities** — Utilities can leverage fund allocation for grid digitalization, smart infrastructure deployment, and clean energy project financing.

**Recommended Actions** — 1. Qualify projects for infrastructure fund allocation. 2. Develop financing structures with KfW and private investors. 3. Build project pipeline aligned with fund priorities.

#### 5. Coal/Gas Retirement 104 GW

**Trend Overview** — The EU retired 11 GW of coal capacity in 2024 alone — a fourfold increase over 2023 — with Germany leading by retiring 6.7 GW [Global Energy Monitor](https://globalenergymonitor.org/report/boom-and-bust-coal-2025/). Most EU countries plan to close all coal plants by 2030, with Germany's coal exit law targeting 23 GW of closures.

**Implications** — Massive plant retirements require site repurposing strategies, workforce transition programs, and replacement capacity planning.

**Opportunities** — Retired plant sites can be repurposed for solar, wind, battery storage, or data center installations.

**Recommended Actions** — 1. Develop site repurposing plans for retiring assets. 2. Create workforce transition programs for affected employees. 3. Evaluate replacement capacity options including renewables and storage.
