# Product Portfolio Consulting Transcript — T-Systems

## Phase 1: Strategic Discovery

### Portfolio Context
- **Company**: T-Systems International GmbH, 28,000 employees, ICT Services
- **Existing state**: 1 product ("Cloud Services") acting as catch-all bucket, 8 features (all cloud-related, all in German), no product_slug on any feature
- **User statement**: T-Systems sells cloud services, managed IT operations, security services, and digital consulting to enterprise customers

### Discovery Analysis

The existing portfolio has a structural problem: everything is stuffed into one product called "Cloud Services." This means the portfolio has zero commercial resolution — a customer looking for security services or IT operations outsourcing would have no entry point. The 8 existing features (sovereign cloud, managed hyperscaler, cloud migration, cloud-native containers, hybrid cloud, multi-cloud FinOps, private cloud, SAP on cloud) are all legitimately cloud capabilities, but they represent only one of the four business lines the user described.

**Key questions I would ask in a live session:**

1. How do customers buy from T-Systems — do they typically start with one service and expand, or do they buy a bundle from day one?
2. Is managed IT operations a standalone profit center or primarily a customer-retention play that keeps clients sticky after a cloud migration?
3. Where does security sit organizationally — is it a standalone business unit with its own P&L, or is security embedded into every other product?
4. What percentage of revenue comes from consulting vs. recurring managed services? Is consulting a door-opener for operations contracts?
5. Do you compete differently in each of these four areas, or is the competitive set largely the same (Atos, Capgemini, Kyndryl)?

**Given the user asked me to challenge their thinking, here is what I observe without needing answers:**

T-Systems is a classic "big IT" company that sells capabilities across a wide spectrum. The danger is defining too many products (which creates go-to-market confusion) or too few (which hides commercial structure). Four products maps cleanly to the four business lines stated.

## Phase 2: Portfolio Shaping — Strategic Lenses

### Maturity Balance
| Product | Maturity | Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| Cloud Services | growth | Core growth engine, well-featured (8 features already defined) |
| Managed IT Operations | mature | Cash cow — large installed base, stable but not growing fast |
| Security Services | growth | Strong tailwind from NIS2, DORA, KRITIS regulations |
| Digital Consulting | growth | Advisory arm that seeds all other product sales |

**Assessment**: The portfolio is commercially balanced. Managed Operations provides stable recurring revenue while Cloud and Security ride secular growth trends. Consulting acts as the tip of the spear. However, there is no concept-stage or development-stage product — nothing representing T-Systems' "next act." This is a risk for a 3-5 year horizon. An AI/automation or edge computing product could fill this gap.

### Customer Journey
The natural land-and-expand motion for T-Systems:

1. **Entry via Consulting**: A CIO engages T-Systems for a cloud strategy or IT modernization assessment (Digital Consulting)
2. **First buy — Cloud Migration**: The strategy engagement leads to a cloud migration project (Cloud Services)
3. **Expand to Managed Operations**: Once workloads are in the cloud, T-Systems operates them (Managed Operations)
4. **Cross-sell Security**: Compliance requirements during migration or operations surface security gaps (Security Services)
5. **Repeat**: New workloads, new business units, new geographies

This is a textbook consulting-to-operations flywheel. The product structure supports it. The risk is that consulting is under-invested because it's seen as a cost center rather than a customer-acquisition channel.

### Competitive Positioning
T-Systems competes against:
- **Hyperscalers directly** (AWS, Azure, GCP) for cloud workloads — but differentiates on sovereignty and managed services
- **Global SIs** (Atos/Eviden, Capgemini, Kyndryl, DXC) for managed operations and consulting
- **Security specialists** (Zscaler, CrowdStrike, Palo Alto) for pure-play security

Having four distinct products (vs. one mega-product) lets T-Systems compete credibly in each space. A single "Cloud Services" product cannot compete against a focused security vendor.

### Pricing Architecture
- Cloud Services: hybrid (subscription base + project migrations)
- Managed Operations: subscription (monthly managed service fees)
- Security Services: hybrid (subscription SOC + project implementations)
- Digital Consulting: project-based (day-rate engagements)

This covers the full spectrum from project-based entry to recurring revenue, which is right for a company this size.

### Differentiation Clarity
Could a new T-Systems employee explain each product in one sentence?
- Cloud Services: "We build and run cloud environments — sovereign, hyperscaler, or hybrid."
- Managed Operations: "We run your IT day-to-day so your team can focus on business."
- Security Services: "We protect your infrastructure and keep you compliant."
- Digital Consulting: "We help you decide what to modernize and how."

Yes — the boundaries are clean and a salesperson could explain the portfolio in 60 seconds.

### Cannibalization Risk
Minimal. The four products serve different buyer needs at different stages of the customer journey. The only tension point: Cloud Services includes "managed hyperscaler" capabilities that could overlap with Managed Operations. I have kept managed hyperscaler as a Cloud Services feature because it is cloud-specific operations (AWS/Azure/GCP expertise), while Managed Operations covers broader IT operations (service desk, end-user computing, network ops). If this distinction proves confusing in practice, consolidating all "managed" capabilities under one product would be the fix.

## Phase 3: Product Structure

### Portfolio Summary

| Slug | Name | Revenue Model | Maturity | Positioning |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| cloud-services | Cloud Services | hybrid | growth | The European enterprise cloud partner — sovereign by design, hyperscaler by capability |
| managed-operations | Managed IT Operations | subscription | mature | Run your IT like a service — 24/7 operations with German engineering rigor |
| security-services | Security Services | hybrid | growth | Security that speaks compliance — built for KRITIS, NIS2, and DORA from day one |
| digital-consulting | Digital Consulting | project | growth | From strategy to execution — consulting that ships, not just presents |

### Strategic Recommendation

**What I would keep:**
- Cloud Services as the flagship product with 8 well-defined features. This is where T-Systems has the deepest differentiation (sovereign cloud, Open Telekom Cloud, SAP partnership) and the feature set is comprehensive.
- The four-product structure maps cleanly to T-Systems' actual go-to-market. It is not artificially granular and not artificially bundled.

**What I would change:**
1. **Add a concept-stage product for AI & Automation** — T-Systems has no "next act" in the portfolio. Every major competitor (Atos, Kyndryl, Capgemini) has an AI/GenAI services product. T-Systems should have at least a concept-stage product here to signal pipeline investment. I did not add it now because the user didn't mention it, but I would advocate for it.
2. **Reposition Digital Consulting from project to hybrid** — If consulting engagements generate retainer-based advisory work (which they often do at this scale), the revenue model should be hybrid, not pure project. This is a conversation to have with the user.
3. **Consider splitting SAP on Cloud into its own product** — SAP is T-Systems' single largest partnership (Platinum Partner, 4,000+ SAP experts, 850,000 managed users). This is not just another cloud workload — it's a distinct buying center with its own budget, its own decision-makers, and its own competitive set (Accenture, Deloitte, IBM). I would seriously consider making SAP a fifth product. I kept it as a feature under Cloud Services for now to stay within the user's four-product framing, but this is the highest-impact structural change I would recommend.

**Customer journey analysis:**
The consulting-to-cloud-to-operations flywheel is sound. The biggest gap is the lack of a self-serve or low-commitment entry point. Every T-Systems engagement requires a sales conversation and a consulting assessment. This is appropriate for the enterprise segment, but it means T-Systems has no way to capture upper-mid-market customers who want to start small. A PoC/PoV tier in Cloud Services or a packaged security assessment in Security Services could fill this gap.

**Competitive angle:**
- Kyndryl sells one mega-product (managed infrastructure) — T-Systems' four-product structure is more navigable
- Capgemini has 10+ service lines — T-Systems' leaner structure is more focused
- The sovereign cloud positioning is T-Systems' unique structural advantage that none of the global SIs can match

**Biggest risk:**
The SAP capability buried as a feature under Cloud Services is the highest-risk structural decision. SAP is T-Systems' most defensible competitive position (Platinum Partner status, 850K managed users). Treating it as just another cloud feature under-represents its commercial weight and makes it invisible in portfolio-level views. If a competitor ran a portfolio analysis, they would see "Cloud Services with 8 features" — not "the largest European SAP operations partner." I would revisit this after the first round of features is complete for the other three products.

**Recommended next steps:**
1. Define features for Security Services — the regulatory tailwind (NIS2, DORA) makes this the highest-urgency product to flesh out
2. Define features for Managed Operations — this is the revenue backbone and needs structure
3. Define features for Digital Consulting — this is smaller but seeds all other sales
4. Revisit whether SAP on Cloud should be promoted to its own product after the full feature landscape is visible
