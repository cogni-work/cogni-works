## 5. Decarbonization & Sustainable Infrastructure Investment

> How do we leverage Carbon Contracts, green financing, and the €500B infrastructure fund for decarbonization and sustainable value creation?

**Executive Sponsor:** CSO / VP Strategy & Sustainability

### Investment Thesis

The financial architecture of energy decarbonization is undergoing a structural transformation that creates unprecedented investment opportunities for German utilities. Germany has announced €6 billion in funding for the 2026 CCfD auction round within a broader programme budget of approximately €50 billion [Hydrogen Insight](https://www.hydrogeninsight.com/policy/hydrogen-in-industry-germany-to-set-aside-roughly-50bn-for-carbon-contracts-for-difference-subsidy-scheme/2-1-1462051), providing 15-year contracts that cover both capital and operational costs for industrial decarbonization projects. The 2026 round significantly expands scope to include CCS, CCU, industrial heat pumps, and hydrogen-based processes [Fuel Cells Works](https://fuelcellsworks.com/2025/10/06/green-investment/germany-opens-pre-bid-phase-for-6bn-2026-climate-contracts-tender-adds-ccs-and-hydrogen-scope), creating new pathways for utility-led decarbonization across hard-to-abate sectors including chemicals, steel, cement, and ceramics.

Simultaneously, the EU ETS2 expands carbon pricing to buildings and road transport from 2028, nearly doubling the share of EU GHG emissions covered by carbon pricing to approximately 75% [Fiegenbaum Solutions](https://www.fiegenbaum.solutions/en/blog/eu-ets2-launch-2028-emissions-trading-buildings-transport). At an anticipated entry price of €60/t CO2, German household heating costs would increase by approximately €17 per year, while Eastern European households face increases of €100-400 per year [Bertelsmann Stiftung](https://www.bertelsmann-stiftung.de/en/topics/latest-news/2026/how-the-price-of-co2-will-affect-heating-costs-in-europe). This pricing signal fundamentally reshapes energy cost structures, creating competitive advantages for utilities offering green energy products and energy efficiency services.

The capital availability is extraordinary. Germany's €500 billion infrastructure fund allocates €100 billion specifically for climate neutrality investments, with €58 billion in planned 2026 spending [Clean Energy Wire](https://www.cleanenergywire.org/factsheets/qa-germanys-eu500-bln-infrastructure-fund-whats-it-climate-and-energy). At the European level, utilities are deploying €173 billion in 2026 CAPEX [ING Think](https://think.ing.com/articles/energy-european-utilities-2026-steady-currents/), while the European Commission estimates €240 billion for hydrogen grid infrastructure to 2040 [PV Magazine](https://www.pv-magazine.com/2025/12/12/the-hydrogen-stream-european-commission-estimates-e240-billion-investments-in-hydrogen-grid-to-2040/). The EU has approved €992 million for 15 renewable hydrogen projects [Carbon Credits](https://carboncredits.com/eu-invests-e992m-in-renewable-hydrogen-projects-for-decarbonization/). However, a critical caution: only 4% of hydrogen projects currently reach Final Investment Decision [Strategic Energy Europe](https://strategicenergy.eu/hydrogen-projects/), indicating that execution capability — not funding availability — is the binding constraint.

The circular economy adds a new value dimension: the global second-life EV battery market is valued at $1.70 billion in 2026 and projected to reach $224 billion by 2040 at 41.72% CAGR [Roots Analysis](https://www.rootsanalysis.com/second-life-ev-battery-market). Repurposed batteries retain 70-80% capacity for grid storage applications, with potential exceeding 200 GWh by 2030 representing $30 billion in value [MarketsandMarkets](https://www.marketsandmarkets.com/Market-Reports/second-life-ev-battery-market-40890194.html). For utilities, this creates a new asset class at the intersection of storage, sustainability, and circular economy regulation.
