# T-Systems Product Portfolio Definition — Strategic Assessment

## What I Found

The existing portfolio only covered cloud sub-products (8 products, all within the cloud pillar). The stated business scope — cloud services, managed IT operations, security services, and digital consulting — was radically under-represented. I have defined 17 products across all four pillars plus strategic adjacencies.

## Strategic Challenges — My Honest Assessment

### 1. The "stuck in the middle" problem
T-Systems is neither the cheapest managed services provider nor the most innovative digital partner. Accenture, Capgemini, and Infosys compete on consulting and transformation. AWS, Azure, and Google compete on platform. T-Systems needs to win on the *intersection* — European sovereignty + operational depth + carrier integration — rather than trying to compete across every dimension.

### 2. The cloud cash cows are under pressure
Private Cloud (VMware/Broadcom licensing) and Managed Infrastructure (secular cloud migration) are both structurally declining. Revenue from these must be actively converted to sovereign cloud and managed hyperscaler revenue. This is not a "someday" problem — it is happening now.

### 3. SAP is a strength and a dependency
850,000 managed SAP users is a remarkable asset. But SAP's own strategy (RISE with SAP, BTP) increasingly pushes customers toward hyperscaler-native deployments that may not require a managed services intermediary. T-Systems must evolve from "SAP hoster" to "SAP transformation partner" to stay relevant.

### 4. Security could be the breakout growth story
Carrier-scale threat intelligence from Telekom's backbone is a genuine, hard-to-replicate moat. As NIS2 and DORA regulations drive security spending in European enterprises, T-Systems/Telekom Security has a window to establish a leading European MSSP position. This deserves disproportionate investment.

### 5. Too many early-stage bets, not enough focus
Data & AI, IoT & Digital Twin, OT Security, and Application Modernization are all marked "early-growth" or "growth." That is four bets competing for investment in a company that historically under-invests in innovation relative to its managed services base. Pick two. Ruthlessly deprioritize the others until the first two reach scale.

### 6. The consulting feeder model only works if consulting is excellent
Digital Consulting is positioned as a feeder for downstream managed services deals. This only works if the consulting quality is genuinely top-tier. If consulting is mediocre, it becomes a cost center that fails to convert. T-Systems should benchmark consulting win rates and downstream conversion honestly.

### 7. Missing: Industry-specific solutions
The portfolio is organized by technology layer (cloud, network, security, consulting). Increasingly, enterprise buyers want industry-specific solutions — "cloud for healthcare," "digital twin for automotive." The hybrid-cloud product hints at this (Health Hybrid Cloud variant), but there is no systematic industry solution packaging. This is a gap competitors are filling.

## Recommended Priority Actions

1. **Double down on Sovereign Cloud + Security** — these are the two products where T-Systems has genuine, structural competitive advantages
2. **Build explicit migration paths** from Private Cloud and Managed Infrastructure into Sovereign Cloud and Managed Hyperscaler
3. **Create 2-3 industry solution bundles** (e.g., "Healthcare Cloud," "Automotive Digital Twin") that cross-cut the technology products
4. **Force-rank the growth bets** — cannot fund four early-stage products equally in a margin-constrained business
5. **Measure consulting-to-managed-services conversion** — if Digital Consulting is not feeding the funnel, restructure it
